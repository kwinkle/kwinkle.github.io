Creating Socially Assistive Robots for the Real World

My research interests are driven by an overall ambition to create autonomous, engaging, socially assistive robots that are effective in the real world. Technically, this means developing robots that can 'read' a complex social situation and act accordingly - e.g. knowing when to challenge you versus when to back off, or even tell a joke. However, it also means thinking about acceptability - robots have got to be used if they're going to be useful; and this is reflected in my choice of design/automation methodologies and the user studies I run.

Working with Domain Experts

Human-human interaction is vital in socially assistive scenarios like healthcare and education, and I want to create robots that support and augment those interactions rather try to replicate or replace them. In addition, it seems obvious that we should try to maximise the fantastic skillset of (non-roboticist) domain experts like therapists and teachers. So, I work to include these experts at every stage of the robot design and development process, whether through participatory design sessions like workshops and focus groups or through novel machine learning approaches that allow non-roboticists to teach robots what to do directly.

Mutual Shaping

Again thinking about building robots that are effective in the real world it's important to recognise that deployment and use of a robot impacts on and is impacted on by the surrounding social environment. This can positive or negative, for example deployment of a robot might generate new, valuable interactions between people that wouldn't have otherwise happened, or have the opposite effect of preventing a vital interaction that should have happened. I take a mutual shaping approach in my work that recognises this two-way interaction between a robot and its broader environment, e.g. by conducting ethnographic studies of robots deployed 'in-the-wild'.
